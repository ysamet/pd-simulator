# M9.5 — The Sweep Layer and Invasion Thresholds

*Companion explainer for milestone M9.5. This document accompanies the M9.5
implementation spec (forthcoming under `docs/specs/`) and the sweep-layer
scoping decision (`docs/DECISIONS.md` #59). Unlike a spec, which freezes as a
historical record once its milestone lands, this explainer is **living
documentation**: it may be revised and improved over time.*

*Audience: a reader who is just beginning to study game theory and the
Prisoner's Dilemma. Every term is defined on first use, and the central ideas
are illustrated with worked examples you can check with a pencil. Sources are
listed in full at the end.*

---

## Abstract

This milestone adds a *sweep* layer to pdsim: a way to run not one simulation
but a whole family of them, each differing by a controlled amount along one
axis, and to summarise the family as a curve. Its first purpose is to answer
*invasion-threshold* questions — how many cooperators must be dropped into a
population of defectors before cooperation stops dying out and instead spreads
to dominate. This is one of the oldest quantitative questions in the study of
cooperation, dating to Axelrod and Hamilton's founding work on the evolution of
reciprocity, and it has a precise modern form in the theory of *fixation* in
finite populations. Here we explain the underlying science, show that the
threshold can be estimated by hand for simple cases, and describe how the sweep
layer measures it empirically for cases too complex to solve on paper. We then
ground each of the layer's design decisions in the published literature it
descends from.

---

## 1. The science: invasion, fixation, and thresholds

### 1.1 The one-shot game and the repeated game

The Prisoner's Dilemma is the standard toy model of the tension between
individual and collective interest. Two players each choose to **cooperate**
(C) or **defect** (D), and the payoffs are arranged so that defecting is
tempting no matter what the other player does, yet mutual cooperation beats
mutual defection. In pdsim's default payoffs a player earns 5 for defecting on
a cooperator (the *temptation*), 3 when both cooperate (the *reward*), 1 when
both defect (the *punishment*), and 0 for cooperating against a defector (the
*sucker's payoff*). We write these as T = 5, R = 3, P = 1, S = 0, and the
defining orderings of a true Prisoner's Dilemma are T > R > P > S (defection
tempts) and 2R > T + S (steady mutual cooperation beats taking turns
exploiting each other).

Played exactly once, a purely self-interested player defects: whatever the
opponent does, defecting scores more. The interesting behaviour appears only
when the same two players meet *repeatedly* and remember each other. Then the
threat of future retaliation can make cooperation pay — a logic Axelrod and
Hamilton called the "shadow of the future" (Axelrod & Hamilton, 1981). This is
why reciprocal strategies such as **Tit for Tat** (cooperate on the first
round, then copy whatever the opponent did last) can hold cooperation together:
a Tit for Tat rewards cooperation with cooperation and answers a defection with
a single defection, so partners who cooperate prosper and partners who cheat
are promptly punished.

### 1.2 From "who wins a game" to "who takes over a population"

The question this milestone is built around is not "who wins a single game" but
"who *takes over a population*." Imagine 100 agents. Most play Always Defect; a
small cluster play Tit for Tat. Each generation, everyone plays everyone,
scores are tallied, and the next generation is repopulated so that
higher-scoring strategies become more common. Over many generations one of two
things happens: the invaders dwindle to zero, or they sweep to 100% of the
population. A strategy reaching the *entire* population is said to have reached
**fixation**, and the central empirical quantity of this milestone is *how the
chance of fixation depends on the size of the starting cluster of invaders*.

### 1.3 A threshold you can find with arithmetic

For simple cases the tipping point can be found by hand, and doing so once
makes the whole idea concrete. Take matches of 10 rounds, "round-robin" play
(every agent plays every other once per generation), and the payoffs above.

Consider the four kinds of encounter:

- A **Tit for Tat against an Always Defect**: the Tit for Tat cooperates on
  round 1 and is suckered (0), then retaliates for the remaining nine rounds,
  earning the mutual-defection payoff of 1 each — a total of 0 + 9×1 = **9**.
  The defector earns 5 on round 1 (temptation) then 1 for nine rounds — a total
  of 5 + 9 = **14**.
- **Two Tit for Tats against each other**: they cooperate every round, earning
  the reward of 3 for ten rounds — a total of **30** each.
- **Two Always Defects against each other**: mutual defection every round,
  1 × 10 = **10** each.

Now suppose the population holds *k* Tit for Tats among 100 − *k* defectors. A
single Tit for Tat plays the other *k* − 1 Tit for Tats (earning 30 each) and
all 100 − *k* defectors (earning 9 each), for a total score of

  30(*k* − 1) + 9(100 − *k*) = 21*k* + 870.

A single defector plays the *k* Tit for Tats (earning 14 each) and the other
99 − *k* defectors (earning 10 each), for a total of

  14*k* + 10(99 − *k*) = 4*k* + 990.

The invaders out-earn the defenders exactly when 21*k* + 870 > 4*k* + 990,
which rearranges to 17*k* > 120, i.e. *k* > 7.06. So **from about 8 invaders
onward, the average Tit for Tat already beats the average defector**, and
selection begins to favour the invaders. Below that cluster size, the invaders
are exploited by defectors faster than their profitable encounters with each
other can compensate; above it, those in-group encounters carry them. That
crossover is the **invasion threshold**, and it is precisely the kind of
quantity the sweep layer traces automatically across a whole range of cluster
sizes.

### 1.4 Why the real threshold is a probability, not a sharp line

The hand calculation above is a *mean-field* shortcut: it compares average
scores as though every agent met the population's exact mixture in exact
proportion. Real populations are finite and stochastic. Who actually meets whom
each generation, and which agents happen to get copied into the next, both
involve chance. As a result, invasion is not a clean yes/no event at a single
cluster size; it is a *probability* that climbs from near zero to near one
across a threshold *region*.

The mathematics of this probabilistic threshold is the theory of fixation in
finite populations. Its modern foundation is the result that a single mutant's
probability of eventually taking over beats the neutral baseline of 1/N (the
chance a strategy fixes by pure luck when it confers no advantage, N being the
population size) exactly when the mutant's fitness exceeds the resident's at a
population frequency of one-third — the so-called "one-third law" (Nowak,
Sasaki, Taylor & Fudenberg, 2004; Taylor, Fudenberg, Sasaki & Nowak, 2004).

Whether that threshold region is a gentle slope or an abrupt cliff is governed
by the **selection intensity** β in pdsim's Fermi update rule — the parameter
the "Drift vs Meritocracy" scenario turns. This rule is not arbitrary: it is
the "pairwise comparison" process borrowed from statistical physics, in which
an inverse temperature β controls how strongly score differences drive who
copies whom, and which spans a single unified range from pure random drift at
β = 0 to near-deterministic imitation at large β (Traulsen, Nowak & Pacheco,
2006). And the shape you can watch the M9.5 preview tool draw — a curve that
sharpens toward a step as β rises — is exactly the predicted behaviour: as
selection strengthens, the fixation-probability curve converges to a step
function whose jump sits at the unstable tipping point between the two outcomes
(Traulsen & Hauert, 2009). Below the tipping point, near-certain extinction of
the invaders; above it, near-certain takeover.

The sweep layer's job is to measure that curve empirically for the many cases —
mixed backgrounds, several invader types at once, execution noise, alternative
selection rules — where no tidy hand calculation exists.

## 2. What the sweep layer builds

A single pdsim run answers "what happened this time?" A **sweep** answers "how
does the outcome change as I turn one knob?" Concretely, a sweep is a *config
generator*: you write one base configuration plus one or more *axes* of
variation, the layer expands that into many fully-formed, individually valid
run configurations, runs them all, and summarises the family as a table and a
curve. Four parts make this work (scoped in DECISIONS #59):

1. **SweepSpec** — a small YAML file describing the config family: the base
   configuration, plus axes such as a parameter grid (e.g. selection intensity
   β over {0.01, 0.1, 1.0}), a list of composition mixtures, and a list of
   random seeds. The full set of combinations is the *cross product* of the
   axes.
2. **A parallel batch runner** (`python -m pdsim.sweep`) that runs the expanded
   configurations across multiple processor cores at once. Whole runs are
   independent of one another, so running many of them in parallel is a pure
   speed win — it makes large experiment campaigns affordable long before any
   single run is made faster.
3. **An Outcome Metrics Registry** — a catalogue of named, documented functions
   that read a finished run and return a number: the invader's final share, a
   fixation flag, time to fixation, cooperation-collapse measures, and so on.
   This is the fourth instance of pdsim's "registry" pattern (after the
   Parameter, Strategy, and Scenario registries): every metric is named,
   described in plain language, and validated, just like every parameter.
4. **Sweep persistence** — a `sweeps/<name>/` folder holding the individual
   member runs, a summary table with one row per run (its axis values and its
   metric values), and a built-in chart of a chosen metric against a chosen
   axis.

### 2.1 Describing the population: the three-bucket composition model

The motivating experiments vary *who is in the population*, so the composition
axis deserves its own vocabulary. Every strategy in a sweep's population falls
into exactly one of three buckets (out of a fixed total population size N):

- **The varying invader (V)** — the strategy whose count we march upward across
  the sweep (for now, one such strategy per sweep; see §3.2).
- **Fixed counts (Cᵢ)** — strategies held at a constant number in every run of
  the sweep (for example, a steady background of 10 Random agents).
- **Percentage fills (Fᵢ)** — strategies that divide up whatever seats remain,
  each taking a fixed percentage Pᵢ of the leftover.

The leftover — the number of seats the fill bucket must divide — is

  R = N − [V + Σ Cᵢ],

and the model is subject to two rules: V + Σ Cᵢ ≤ N (so R is never negative —
you cannot seat more agents than exist), and Σ Pᵢ = 1 (the fill percentages
account for exactly all of the remainder). The pleasing consequence for
invasion experiments is that as V grows, R shrinks, so the background society
keeps its internal *character* fixed even as its total *size* contracts — the
invader grows against a society of constant composition but diminishing number.

### 2.2 Whole agents: largest-remainder rounding, worked out

Percentages are continuous, but agents are discrete: you cannot seat 29.4 of
them. And because R changes at every step of the sweep, the percentages almost
never divide evenly. The layer therefore uses the **largest-remainder rule**
(the same arithmetic used to allocate a fixed number of legislative seats to
regions by population): give each fill strategy the whole-number *floor* of its
ideal share, then hand out the leftover seats one at a time to the strategies
with the largest fractional parts, breaking ties by a fixed order (alphabetical
machine name) so the result is perfectly reproducible.

A worked example. Take N = 100, invader V = Tit for Tat, no fixed strategies,
and a fill of 30% Always Defect, 30% Always Cooperate, 40% Generous Tit for
Tat. At the sweep step where V = 2, the remainder is R = 98. The ideal shares
are 0.30 × 98 = 29.4, 0.30 × 98 = 29.4, and 0.40 × 98 = 39.2. Their floors are
29, 29, and 39, which sum to 97 — one seat short of 98. The fractional parts
are 0.4 (Always Defect), 0.4 (Always Cooperate), and 0.2 (Generous Tit for
Tat). The single leftover seat goes to the largest fractional part; Always
Defect and Always Cooperate tie at 0.4, so the fixed alphabetical order awards
it to `always_cooperate`. The resolved background is therefore Always Cooperate
30, Always Defect 29, Generous Tit for Tat 39 — a total of 98 — plus the 2
invaders, exactly 100, as close to the requested 30/30/40 as 98 whole seats
permit. (One honest caveat: at small N a single seat is a larger fraction of
the whole, so the background ratio can jitter by a percent or two from step to
step. This is a property of integer populations, not a defect to engineer
around.)

### 2.3 The load-bearing principle: a generator, never a weakener

Every configuration the sweep expands is a *first-class config that could have
been written by hand*, and it passes the platform's full validation — the same
range checks, payoff-ordering checks, and composition-sum checks any config
faces. The percentages, the fixed counts, and the rounding rule live only in
the SweepSpec; what gets written into each member run's own `config.yaml` is
the *resolved integer composition*. This keeps pdsim's reproducibility contract
intact: any single run in a sweep can be re-run from its own folder alone, with
no knowledge of the sweep, the percentages, or the rounding rule that produced
it. The sweep layer generates configs; it never leaves a member run depending
on it.

## 3. Design decisions, grounded in the literature

This section records the choices that shape what the milestone can express and
measure, each with its rationale and the published work it descends from or
deliberately departs from.

### 3.1 The invasion-threshold question is the organising goal

The layer's first research program — how large a cluster of reciprocators must
be to invade a population of defectors — is the modern, empirical form of a
question as old as the field. Axelrod and Hamilton (1981) showed that although
a lone reciprocator cannot invade a world of defectors (it is exploited before
it can find a partner), a *cluster* of reciprocators can, because its members
interact with one another often enough to prosper. The threshold form — that
success requires the cluster to exceed a critical initial frequency, and that
this critical frequency grows with the size of the interacting group — is a
recurring result in the reciprocity literature. The sweep layer is the tool
that measures such thresholds for pdsim's populations directly.

### 3.2 One varying invader now, architecture ready for several

M9.5 restricts a sweep to a single varying invader (V is one strategy). This
covers the founding single-invader research program completely and keeps the
composition model simple. But the three-bucket formalism is written so that V
is conceptually a *set*: allowing two simultaneous invaders later means only
letting V hold more than one entry, with the constraint reading Σ Vⱼ + Σ Cᵢ ≤ N
and the fill logic untouched. This forward-compatibility is deliberate, because
the owner's longer research arc targets *ethnocentrism* dynamics — the
coexistence and competition of in-group-cooperating "tag" species (Hammond &
Axelrod's model, the subject of the later M12 milestone) — where varying two
invading species against each other is the natural experiment. Building the
composition model as a bucket-of-invaders now avoids reworking it then.

### 3.3 Fixation is defined as an instantaneous event

A metric must define fixation precisely. The layer defines it as an
*instantaneous* event: the invader reaching 100% of the population at any
generation, recorded at the *first* such generation. It deliberately does not
require the population to *stay* fixed. The reason is that under mutation (the
small chance μ that a new agent adopts a random strategy rather than copying a
successful one), a "fixed" population can lose its purity a generation later, so
"fixed and held forever" is not a stable notion once μ > 0. This is not a pdsim
quirk but a known feature of finite-population dynamics with mutation: in the
small-mutation regime the population spends its time near the pure states and
occasionally hops between them (Fudenberg & Imhof, 2006; Fudenberg, Nowak,
Taylor & Imhof, 2006). Accordingly, the layer offers **strict fixation** as the
right measure for mutation-free (μ = 0) sweeps, and **quasi-fixation** measures
— "ever exceeded x%", "held above x% for k consecutive generations", "mean
share over the last k generations" — as the meaningful measures when μ > 0,
where strict fixation is unstable and staying-power is the honest question.

### 3.4 Time to fixation is recorded with censoring

Some runs reach fixation; others end first. Reporting "never" for the second
group would be wrong — the run simply stopped before the story finished. The
layer therefore borrows the standard bookkeeping of *survival analysis*: each
run records a fixation *time* and a *censored* flag. If the invader fixed, the
time is the generation it first did so and the run is uncensored; if the run
ended first, the time is the number of generations completed and the run is
marked censored (fixation might still have happened later, unobserved). This
two-column encoding — rather than a sentinel value like −1 or infinity — keeps
the data honest and directly usable by standard survival-curve analysis, and it
matters because fixation *times* in finite populations can be long and are
themselves objects of study (Taylor, Fudenberg, Sasaki & Nowak, 2004).

### 3.5 Replicates are a first-class axis, because the outcome is random

Because invasion is a probability rather than a certainty (§1.4), a single run
at a given cluster size does not estimate the threshold — it draws one sample
from a coin whose bias is what we actually want to know. The layer therefore
treats the random seed as an ordinary sweep axis: each cluster size is run many
times with different seeds, and the metric of interest (say, the fraction of
replicates that reached fixation, or the mean final invader share) is an average
over those repeats, reported with its spread. This is the direct practical
consequence of taking the finite-population, stochastic view of evolution
seriously (Nowak, Sasaki, Taylor & Fudenberg, 2004; Traulsen, Nowak & Pacheco,
2006) rather than the infinite-population, deterministic idealisation.

### 3.6 Selection intensity β is a primary sweep axis

The sharpness of the threshold is not a nuisance to be averaged away; it is
itself a finding. pdsim's default Fermi selection rule copies strategies with a
probability that depends on score differences scaled by the selection intensity
β — the inverse "temperature" of the pairwise-comparison process from
statistical physics, which interpolates smoothly from pure drift at β = 0 to
deterministic imitation at large β (Traulsen, Nowak & Pacheco, 2006). Sweeping
β turns the threshold curve from a gentle slope into an abrupt cliff, tracing
the theoretical prediction that strong selection converges the
fixation-probability curve to a step function at the unstable interior fixed
point (Traulsen & Hauert, 2009). Making β a headline axis lets an experimenter
see drift and meritocracy as two ends of one continuum rather than two separate
worlds.

### 3.7 Metrics are pure post-processing over recorded runs

Every metric is computed from a *recorded* run rather than during simulation,
reading the loaded time series rather than the raw storage files directly. Two
benefits follow. First, metrics apply *retroactively*: a metric written today
works on any run recorded since the platform first gained persistence, because
it consumes the same reconstructed data structure the live charts use. Second,
metrics inherit the platform's schema-compatibility handling for free rather
than re-implementing it, so a metric written against today's recordings keeps
working as the storage format evolves. This mirrors pdsim's existing
"raw-versus-derived" discipline: store only raw truth, recompute every derived
view on load.

## 4. Reading the outputs

Unlike a single simulation, which you watch live in the Streamlit app, a sweep
is an inherently *headless* batch job: you launch it from the command line with
`python -m pdsim.sweep <spec.yaml>`, and it writes its results to a
`sweeps/<name>/` folder. (Authoring and launching a sweep *from within the
app* — a Sweep tab that builds the `spec.yaml` for you and starts the batch
job — arrives in the next increment, M9.5b; the milestone was split so that
the headless core could be built and validated first. The *execution* stays
headless either way: the tab will simply write the same spec file and launch
the same runner.) That folder holds three things worth understanding.

The **summary table** (`sweep_summary.parquet`) has one row per member run.
Each row carries the run's identity, its axis values (the invader count, the β
value, the seed, and so on), and its metric values (fixation flag, time to
fixation with its censored flag, final share, and any others the spec
requested). This table is the raw material for any analysis you want to do
afterwards.

The **metric-versus-axis chart** is the built-in headline artifact, and it is
exactly the picture the in-chat preview tool lets you explore now: a chosen
metric (say, the invader's final share, or the fraction of replicates that
reached fixation) on the vertical axis, a chosen axis (say, the invader's
starting count) on the horizontal, with a shaded band showing the spread across
replicate seeds at each point. An invasion threshold shows up as the
characteristic shape you have already seen: a flat floor at low invader counts
(the invaders reliably die out), a sharp rise through the threshold region, and
a flat ceiling at high counts (they reliably take over). Turning up the
selection intensity β and re-running sharpens that rise toward a vertical cliff;
turning it down softens it toward a gentle ramp — the drift-to-meritocracy
continuum made visible.

The **member runs** themselves are ordinary pdsim run folders, so any single
one can be opened in the app's Results browser and inspected in full detail —
its composition trajectory, its score and cooperation charts — exactly like a
run you launched by hand. (Interactive browsing of a *whole sweep* inside the
app, rather than run-by-run, is a deliberately deferred, later increment; in
M9.5 the sweep's own summary is the parquet table and the metric-versus-axis
chart.)

For building intuition before running anything, the M9.5 preview tool is the
best current instrument: its top panel shows the exact composition arithmetic
of §2 (watch the three buckets and largest-remainder rounding respond as you
edit N, the invader range, and the fill percentages), and its bottom panel
runs a faithful stand-in for the engine to trace the threshold curve of §1.4
and §3.6, so you can feel how β, match length, and mutation move the tipping
point.
